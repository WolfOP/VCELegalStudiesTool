Unit 4: The People, the Law and Reform

This unit explores the institutions that make and reform laws in Australia, and the ability of individuals and groups to influence these processes.

Area of Study 1: The People and the Law-makers

This area of study focuses on how the Australian Constitution establishes law-making powers and acts as a check on parliament, the significance of the High Court, and the roles of parliament and courts in law-making. You will explore their relationship and the factors affecting their ability to make law.

This section explores the roles of the Crown and Houses of Parliament in law-making, the division of law-making powers, the significance of s109, and the impact of High Court cases [cite: 625-630].

Roles of Crown & Houses of Parliament (Vic & Cth)
  • Commonwealth Parliament: King (rep. by Governor-General), Senate (Upper House), House of Representatives (Lower House).
  • Victorian Parliament: King (rep. by Governor), Legislative Council (Upper House), Legislative Assembly (Lower House).
  • Lower Houses (House of Reps/Legislative Assembly): Initiate laws (especially money bills), form government, represent people, scrutinise government.
  • Upper Houses (Senate/Legislative Council): Act as house of review, scrutinise bills, represent states/regions, can initiate non-money bills [cite: 405-406].
  • The Crown (Governor-General/Governor): Grants Royal Assent to bills for them to become law.
Division of Law-Making Powers
  • Exclusive Powers: Exercised only by Commonwealth Parliament (e.g., defence, currency, customs).
  • Concurrent Powers: Shared by Commonwealth and State Parliaments (e.g., taxation, marriage, trade).
  • Residual Powers: Powers remaining with State Parliaments, not listed in Constitution (e.g., criminal law, education, public transport).
Significance of Section 109

If a state law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law in an area of concurrent power, the Commonwealth law prevails, and the state law is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency. This requires a court challenge.

High Court Cases Impacting Law-Making Powers

Cases like R v Brislan (broadened Cth power over communications [cite: 93-95, 423]) and Commonwealth v Tasmania (Tasmanian Dams Case) (Cth can legislate on matters of international treaties, impacting state residual powers [cite: 96-99, 424]) have significantly affected the division of powers.

Parliament's effectiveness in law-making is influenced by several factors.

  • Bicameral Structure: A 'hostile upper house' (government lacks majority) can increase scrutiny but slow law-making. A 'rubber stamp' (government controls both houses) can mean less scrutiny but faster law-making [cite: 371, 520-521].
  • International Pressures: Treaties and international organisations (e.g., UN) can pressure parliament to make laws complying with international standards [cite: 254, 373-374, 452]. Failure to comply can lead to criticism.
  • Representative Nature of Parliament (Representative Government): Parliament should reflect community views (s7 & s24 of Constitution). Regular elections, political parties, and independents influence this.

The Australian Constitution acts as a check on parliamentary law-making power through several mechanisms [cite: 632-633, 427, 544].

  • Role of the High Court in Protecting Representative Government: The High Court interprets ss 7 and 24 of the Constitution to protect representative government, including an implied right to freedom of political communication (ACTV case) [cite: 62, 594-595] and defining voting rights (Roach v Electoral Commissioner [cite: 247, 317-318, 597]).
  • Separation of Powers: Divides power between legislative (parliament), executive (government [cite: 64, 234, 531-533]), and judicial (courts [cite: 65, 235, 534-535]) branches to prevent abuse. The independent judiciary can declare laws ultra vires (Australian Communist Party v Commonwealth). Overlap exists between legislative and executive.
  • Express Protection of Rights: Five rights are explicitly stated (e.g., s51(xxxi) acquisition of property on just terms, s116 freedom of religion). These are entrenched and can be enforced by the High Court, but are limited in number and scope.
  • Bicameral Structure: (Covered under 'Factors Affecting Parliament') The need for bills to pass both houses acts as a check.
  • Requirement for a Double Majority in a Referendum (s128): Changing the Constitution requires approval by a national majority of voters and a majority of voters in a majority of states. This makes constitutional change difficult.

Courts make law through statutory interpretation and the doctrine of precedent [cite: 634-635, 392].

Statutory Interpretation

Courts give meaning to words in legislation when applying it to a case. Reasons include clarifying ambiguity (Deing v Tarola), reflecting changing word nature (A-G v Kevin and Jennifer), and applying to unforeseen circumstances. Effects include creating precedent, broadening/narrowing statutes, and prompting legislative change.

Doctrine of Precedent

Judges follow reasons for decisions (ratio decidendi [cite: 255, 437, 850-851]) of superior courts in the same hierarchy for similar cases (stare decisis). Features include: binding precedent (must follow), persuasive precedent (can guide, e.g., obiter dictum, decisions from other hierarchies). Ways of developing/avoiding: reversing (same case on appeal), overruling (different later case by superior court), distinguishing (material facts different), disapproving (lower court disagrees but follows).

Refer to 'Case Explorer' for examples.

Several factors can influence or limit the courts' law-making role.

  • Doctrine of Precedent: While providing consistency, it also restricts judges as they are bound by higher court decisions and must wait for a relevant case.
  • Judicial Conservatism and Judicial Activism: Conservatism is a reluctance to make significant new law, deferring to parliament. Activism is a willingness to develop common law considering social/political factors, which can lead to major changes but may be seen as overstepping.
  • Costs and Time: Litigation can be expensive and lengthy, deterring individuals and limiting cases that come before courts.
  • Requirement for Standing (Locus Standi): Parties must have a 'special interest' and be directly affected by an issue to bring a case [cite: 35-36, 261, 462, 889]. This prevents frivolous cases but can also stop public interest matters from being heard.

Courts and Parliament have a dynamic and complementary relationship in law-making.

  • Supremacy of Parliament: Parliament is the supreme law-making body and can make or change any law within its constitutional powers, including overriding common law (except High Court constitutional interpretations).
  • Ability of Courts to Influence Parliament: Judicial decisions, statutory interpretations, and obiter dicta can highlight legal issues or gaps, prompting Parliament to consider law reform.
  • Codification of Common Law: Parliament can enact legislation that incorporates and gives statutory authority to common law principles established by courts (e.g., Mabo case and the Native Title Act 1993 (Cth)).
  • Abrogation of Common Law: Parliament can pass legislation that overrides or cancels common law decisions made by courts (unless the court decision is based on constitutional interpretation by the High Court). E.g., Wrongs (Animals Straying on Highways) Act 1984 (Vic) abrogated the common law position in Trigwell's case.

Abrogation of Common Law: The process of parliament overruling common law by creating a statute that is contrary to a decision of the courts. Parliament has the power to pass legislation that overrides most common law decisions, with the exception of High Court decisions on constitutional matters.
Acquisition of Property on Just Terms: An express right under section 51(xxxi) of the Australian Constitution. This section gives the Commonwealth Parliament the power to legislate about and acquire 'property on just terms from any State or person' for any purpose within its law-making powers. If this power is exercised, the Commonwealth must provide 'just terms', such as fair and reasonable compensation. Limitations exist, such as no specification on what constitutes 'just terms' and this section not applying to the states.
Act of Parliament (Legislation/Statute Law): A law made by parliament. A bill becomes an Act of Parliament after it has been agreed to by both houses of parliament and signed by the Governor-General (Commonwealth) or Governor (Victorian).
Aims (of Study): VCE Legal Studies aims include enabling students to understand and apply legal terminology and principles, apply legal principles to scenarios, analyse law-making institutions and influences on law reform, understand legal rights, analyse dispute resolution methods, and examine the justice system's ability to achieve the principles of justice.
Australian Constitution: A set of rules that establishes the nature, functions, powers, duties, and limits of government in Australia. It establishes the Commonwealth Parliament and High Court, sets out matters relating to the states, defines Commonwealth law-making powers, and provides a mechanism (referendum) to change it. It establishes law-making powers of state and Commonwealth parliaments and protects the Australian people through structures that check parliament in law-making.
Balance of Power: The support of minor parties or independents in a house of parliament that enables the government to form a majority and enact their law-making. This occurs when the government does not hold a majority in the upper house (hostile upper house).
Bicameral Parliament/Structure: A law-making body with two houses or chambers that must approve new bills or amendments to laws. The requirement for a bicameral structure is laid out in the respective constitutions of the Victorian and Commonwealth Parliaments. The inclusion of a second house can prevent the government from passing legislation that aligns solely with its political agenda.
Bill: A proposed law introduced in a parliament by a member of one of its houses. To become a law, a bill must be agreed to by both houses of parliament and signed by the Governor-General (Commonwealth) or Governor (Victorian).
Binding Precedent: Legal reasoning of a higher court that must be followed by all lower courts in the same court hierarchy where the material facts are similar. If a precedent is binding, a judge must follow it regardless of whether they agree with the legal reasoning.
Broadening and Narrowing Statutes: Effects of statutory interpretation. Courts can broaden the meaning of legislation, expanding its scope, or narrow the meaning, restricting the scope of the law.
Cabinet: A central decision-making body consisting of the prime minister (federal) and premier (state), alongside senior ministers, responsible for developing government policy and addressing issues of concern. Cabinet exercises executive powers in practice.
Codification of Common Law: When parliament makes laws to affirm previous decisions within common law. Parliament has the power to do this as it is the supreme law-maker.
Commonwealth Parliament: The federal bicameral parliament in Australia, consisting of the King (represented by the Governor-General), the Senate (upper house), and the House of Representatives (lower house).
Concurrent Powers: Law-making powers that are shared by both the Commonwealth and state parliaments. These are listed in section 51 of the Australian Constitution. Examples include taxation, marriage, and divorce.
Constitution: A set of rules and principles that establishes the nature, functions, powers, duties, and limits of the government. Australia has a formal written constitution.
Constitutional Monarchy: A system of government in which the Crown is the Head of State, but elected representatives have the power to create laws.
Constitutional Reform: The process of changing the Australian Constitution. This requires a referendum.
Constituent: A person who votes for, and is then represented in government by, an elected official.
Costs and Time (Courts): Factors that affect the ability of courts to make law. Pursuing a claim in court can be very time-consuming due to case backlogs and preparation time. Costs can include legal representation fees, court fees, and jury fees. High costs can deter potential plaintiffs from initiating civil proceedings.
Crown: The British monarch, part of the Australian system of government through representatives (Governor-General at federal level, Governors at state level). The Crown's representatives grant royal assent and appoint the Executive Council.
Crossbench: The members of parliament who do not belong to the major political parties, who are either independents or members of minor political parties.
Debate (Parliament): Bills are debated in parliament during the second reading stage.
Delegated Legislation (Secondary Legislation): Rules and regulations made by secondary authorities (local councils, government departments, statutory authorities) that have the power to do so because of an enabling Act passed by parliament.
Democratic Society: An organised system of people living in a community, in which the laws and processes that govern people’s lives are created by elected representatives.
Disapproving a Precedent: An act whereby a lower court expresses its disapproval of a precedent established by a higher court through its written judgment, but is still bound to follow it. Disapproving a precedent does not change it.
Distinguishing a Precedent: A process by which a court can avoid following a binding precedent by identifying a sufficiently large difference between the material facts of the case at hand and the case where the precedent was set.
Division of Powers: The constitutional division of law-making powers between the Commonwealth and state parliaments. It divides powers into exclusive, concurrent, and residual. This is important to avoid abuses of power and share law-making responsibility.
Doctrine of Precedent: A rule in which judges must follow the reasons (ratio decidendi) for decisions given by superior courts in the same court hierarchy when deciding a case before them with similar material facts. It is based on the principle of 'stare decisis'.
Double Majority: A requirement for a referendum to be successful. It requires a majority of voters nationwide in favour, and also a majority of voters in a majority of states (at least four).
Election: A public voting process in which new representatives are chosen to be part of parliament, and from which a government is determined.
Electorate: A geographical area comprised of approximately 110,000 voters represented by a member of the Commonwealth Parliament. In Victoria, an electoral district has 46,000-56,000 eligible voters for the Legislative Assembly.
Employability Skills: The study offers opportunities for students to develop these, with specific examples provided in the Support materials.
Executive Council: A group of senior government ministers (including the prime minister/premier) with the role of advising the Crown’s representative on government matters, and approving secondary legislation.
Executive Power: The power vested in the King and exercised by the Governor-General (at the Commonwealth level), or Governor (at the state level), to maintain and administer the law and the business of government. In practice, this power rests with Cabinet and government departments.
Exclusive Powers: Law-making powers granted only to the Commonwealth Parliament by section 51 and section 52 of the Australian Constitution. State parliaments cannot make laws in these areas. Examples include currency, defence, and customs.
Express Protection of Rights: Rights that are explicitly stated (enumerated) and entrenched in the Australian Constitution. They can only be changed by a referendum. The five express rights are acquisition of property on just terms, trial by jury for Commonwealth indictable offences, interstate trade and commerce, freedom of religion, and no discrimination based on state residence.
External Affairs Power: Law-making power of the Commonwealth Parliament under section 51(xxix) of the Australian Constitution, allowing it to make laws with respect to external affairs. This power has been interpreted by the High Court to allow the Commonwealth to legislate in areas covered by international treaties.
Factors Affecting Parliament's Ability to Make Law: Include the bicameral structure of parliament, international pressures, and the representative nature of parliament.
Factors Affecting Courts' Ability to Make Law: Include the doctrine of precedent, judicial conservatism and activism, costs and time in bringing a case to court, and the requirement for standing.
Fairness: A principle of justice where all people can participate in the justice system, and its processes should be impartial and open.
Freedom of Religion: An express right under section 116 of the Australian Constitution. The Commonwealth shall not make any law for establishing any religion, imposing religious observance, or prohibiting the free exercise of religion. Limitations exist, as it primarily limits the Commonwealth, not state parliaments.
Governor (Victorian): The Crown’s representative in the Victorian Parliament. The primary role is to grant royal assent.
Governor-General (Commonwealth): The Crown’s representative in the Commonwealth Parliament. The primary role is to grant royal assent.
High Court of Australia (HCA): Established by the Australian Constitution. It has jurisdiction under section 75 and section 76 to hear matters arising under the Constitution or involving its interpretation. The HCA acts as a guardian of the Constitution, interprets its meaning, settles disputes, and can declare laws invalid (ultra vires) if they go beyond parliament's law-making powers.
Hostile Upper House: Occurs when the government of the day does not hold a majority in the upper house, and the balance of power is held by the opposition, minor parties, and independents. This increases scrutiny on bills but can slow the law-making process.
House of Representatives (Commonwealth Lower House): The lower house of the Commonwealth Parliament, comprised of 151 members representing electorates across Australia. The political party or coalition with the majority of seats forms the government. Roles include initiating most new bills (especially money bills), determining the government, representing the people, and scrutinising government administration.
Implied Right: A right that is not explicitly outlined in the Australian Constitution but has been interpreted by the High Court to exist. An example is the implied right to freedom of political communication, interpreted from sections 7 and 24.
Initiating Legislation: The process of introducing a proposed law (bill) into parliament. Most legislation is initiated in the lower house. Money bills can only be initiated in the lower house.
International Declarations: Non-binding agreements between two or more countries that establish aspirational rights and obligations. Unlike treaties, they are not subject to ratification. Despite not being legally bound, parliament may face international criticism for failing to uphold the principles set out in a declaration.
International Pressures: The influence that other countries and international organisations exert on parliaments to ensure the laws created comply with international standards. Pressures can come from other countries, intergovernmental organisations (like the UN), or non-governmental organisations.
International Treaties: Binding agreements between two or more countries or international organisations that create international rights and obligations. A country becomes bound after signing and ratifying the treaty. Parliament must pass legislation to give effect to treaty obligations within domestic law.
Interstate Trade and Commerce: An express right under section 92 of the Australian Constitution. This section states that 'trade, commerce, and intercourse among the States... shall be absolutely free'. This is not an absolute right, and laws can restrict interstate movement if they are not discriminatory.
Judicial Activism: Refers to the willingness of judges to consider a range of social and political factors and their own opinions when interpreting the law and making decisions. Those who disapprove argue judges should not make substantial changes as they are not democratically elected.
Judicial Conservatism: An expression used when judges adopt a narrow interpretation of the law, avoiding major or controversial changes and not being influenced by political beliefs or community views. Conservative judges tend to interpret legislation narrowly.
Judicial Power: The power vested in courts and tribunals that enables them to enforce laws and resolve legal matters. At the Commonwealth level, this power is vested predominantly in the High Court. The judiciary must operate independently from the legislative and executive branches.
Juries: Have a role in criminal trials and civil trials. In a criminal trial, the jury determines the guilt of the accused.
Just Terms: Fair and reasonable compensation provided by the Commonwealth Parliament when acquiring property under section 51(xxxi).
Legislative Assembly (Victorian Lower House): The lower house of the Victorian Parliament, comprised of 88 elected members representing electoral districts across Victoria. The political party with the majority of seats forms the government, led by the premier. Roles include initiating most new legislation (especially financial spending legislation), representing the people, reviewing legislation from the Legislative Council, and scrutinising government administration.
Legislative Council (Victorian Upper House): The upper house of the Victorian Parliament, comprised of 40 elected members representing eight regions across Victoria. It is often referred to as the 'house of review'. Roles include acting as a house of review, scrutinising government administration, and initiating bills (other than money bills).
Legislative Power: The power vested in parliament that enables it to make laws. At the Commonwealth level, this power is vested in the Commonwealth Parliament.
Locus Standi (Standing): The requirement that a party must be directly affected by or have a 'special interest' in an issue to bring a case to court.
Material Facts: The key facts or details in a legal case that were critical to the decision.
Member of Parliament (MP): An elected, political representative of people who live in a certain area, such as a district or state.
Minor Parties: Political parties that do not have elected representatives to win government but can place pressure on the government and target specific areas of law reform. They are small political parties.
Money Bills: Bills that relate to government expenditure or taxation. In the Commonwealth Parliament, all money bills must be initiated in the House of Representatives. In the Victorian Parliament, the Legislative Council is prevented from introducing legislation concerned with spending government funds.
Obiter Dictum: Latin term meaning 'said by the way'. These are comments made by the judge that do not necessarily contribute to the ratio decidendi but can be used as persuasive precedent. Disapproving a precedent is an obiter dictum.
Overruling a Precedent: Occurs when a different case is brought before a superior court at a later date, and they change the precedent set by a lower court or one of the same standing in an earlier case.
Parliament: A formal assembly of elected representatives that gathers to make laws for the people. Parliament is the supreme law-making body within its jurisdiction.
Parliamentary Counsel: Lawyers tasked with writing up legislation on the instructions and advice of members of parliament. They aim to draft legislation broadly to cover future circumstances but specifically enough to remove ambiguity.
Persuasive Precedent: Legal reasoning that can act as a guide for judges even though they are not bound to follow it. Examples include obiter dictum, decisions from lower courts or courts in different hierarchies.
Petitions: A formal written request to the government to take some course of action or implement specific law reform, usually with collected signatures. They are the only way individuals can present concerns 'directly' before parliament.
Political Party: An organisation comprised of members of similar ethos and worldviews with the primary aim to compete in elections to achieve societal and political goals.
Premier (Victorian): The leader of the Victorian state government.
Presumption of Innocence: A fundamental principle of criminal law that an accused is presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond reasonable doubt.
Principles of Justice: For VCE Legal Studies, these are fairness, equality, and access.
Fairness (Principle of Justice): All people can participate in the justice system, and its processes should be impartial and open.
Equality (Principle of Justice): All people engaging with the justice system and its processes should be treated the same way; if the same treatment creates disparity or disadvantage, adequate measures should be implemented.
Access (Principle of Justice): All people should be able to engage with the justice system and its processes on an informed basis.
Promoting Legislative Change: An effect of statutory interpretation. Parliament may amend legislation in response to statutory interpretation to provide clarity or confirm a court decision.
Proportional Representation: The Senate is elected by proportional representation. The proportional voting system allows a number of people to be elected from the same region or state, meaning each candidate needs relatively fewer votes to win a seat.
Quantum Translocator: A hypothetical teleportation device used in an example to illustrate statutory interpretation.
Ratio Decidendi: Latin phrase meaning 'the reason for the decision'. This is the legal reason for the decision and is the binding part of a judgment that must be followed by lower courts.
Ratification: The formal agreement or undertaking by a country to be bound by the terms of an international treaty.
Reasons for Statutory Interpretation: Include clarifying the specific meaning of words, the changing nature of words, and applying legislation to unforeseen circumstances.
Referendum: A compulsory national vote on a proposed change to the wording of the Australian Constitution, as outlined in section 128. A double majority is required for success.
Region (Victorian Legislative Council): One of the eight, larger divisions of Victoria that Legislative Council members are elected to represent.
Remand: The legal status of an accused when they are held in custody awaiting trial.
Representative Government: A system of government wherein the people choose elected representatives to parliament to make laws on their behalf. The principle is established and protected by sections 7 and 24 of the Australian Constitution.
Representative Nature of Parliament: Parliament's duty to voters and the community to represent their interests through law-making, underpinned by the election system. It is affected by who is nominated for election and the diversity of parliament.
Residual Powers: Law-making powers that were left solely with the state parliaments at federation. They are not explicitly listed in the Australian Constitution. Examples include criminal law, medical procedures, road laws, education, and public transport.
Responsible Government: The principle that the party or coalition with a majority in the lower house forms the government, and the executive council is responsible to parliament.
Reversing a Precedent: Occurs when a case is appealed and the superior court changes the precedent set by a lower court. A new precedent is established by the superior court. This differs from overruling, which happens in a different case.
Royal Assent: The formal and final approval granted by the monarchy, or its representative (Governor-General or Governor), for a proposed piece of legislation. It is a necessary final step for a bill to become law.
Rubber Stamp (Upper House): A situation where the government holds a majority of seats in both the upper and lower houses, meaning bills may not be scrutinised properly. Bills introduced in the lower house are unlikely to be contested by the upper house.
Scope of Study: VCE Legal Studies examines the institutions and principles essential to the Australian legal system, including the rule of law, law-makers, legal institutions, the relationship between people and the Constitution, the protection of rights, and the Victorian justice system.
Scrutinising Government/Legislation: The process of closely examining bills or government administration. In the Commonwealth Parliament, the Senate's primary role is to scrutinise bills from the House of Representatives. In the Victorian Parliament, the Legislative Council's primary role is also to act as a house of review.
Secondary Legislation: See Delegated Legislation.
Section 109: A section of the Australian Constitution designed to resolve inconsistencies between state and Commonwealth laws in areas of concurrent law-making powers. If a state law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law, the latter prevails to the extent of the inconsistency, and the state law is invalid to that extent. It can act as a restriction on state parliaments.
Sections 7 and 24: Sections of the Australian Constitution that establish the composition of the Senate and the House of Representatives, stipulating that members are to be 'directly chosen by the people' through regular elections. They enforce the principle of representative government.
Senate (Commonwealth Upper House): The upper house of the Commonwealth Parliament, comprised of 76 elected members (12 from each state, 2 from each territory). It is often referred to as the 'states' house' or the house of review. Roles include acting as a house of review, allowing for equal representation of states, scrutinising bills, and initiating bills (other than money bills).
Separation of Powers: A principle established by the Australian Constitution that ensures the legislative, executive, and judicial powers remain separate. It aims to prevent abuses of power. In Australia, the legislative and executive powers overlap in practice, but the judiciary is independent.
Significance of High Court Cases: Refers to the impact a High Court case interpreting the Australian Constitution has on state and Commonwealth law-making powers. Examples include the Brislan case (wireless sets/telecommunications) and the Tasmanian Dams case (external affairs/international treaties).
Significance of Referendums: Refers to the impact a referendum has on changing the Australian Constitution. An example is the 1967 referendum which changed sections 51(xxvi) and removed 127, altering law-making powers regarding Indigenous Australians.
Special Interest: A requirement for a party to have standing in court. The party must be affected by the issue more than any member of the general public.
Standing: The requirement that, in order to bring a case to court, an individual or group must be affected by, or have a special interest in, the issues involved in the case.
Stare Decisis: Latin term meaning 'to stand by what has been decided'. It is a basic principle underlying the doctrine of precedent.
Statute Law: See Act of Parliament.
Statutory Interpretation: A process whereby the courts give meaning to the words in legislation when applying the legislation to a case. Judges interpret statutes to resolve cases, clarify ambiguous words, reflect changing meanings, and apply laws to unforeseen circumstances. It establishes a precedent for future cases.
Supremacy of Parliament: The legal concept that parliament has the freedom to make, amend, or abolish laws (subject to constitutional limitations) and is supreme over other arms of government. Parliament can confirm or cancel common law decisions (except High Court constitutional decisions).
Trial by Jury (Commonwealth Indictable Offences): An express right under section 80 of the Australian Constitution. It requires that the trial on indictment for any offence against a Commonwealth law shall be by jury. This right is limited, as it only applies to Commonwealth indictable offences, not state ones or summary offences.
Ultra Vires: Latin term meaning 'beyond the powers'. Used to describe an act by a government body or corporation that requires legal authority but is done without it. Courts may declare laws ultra vires if they go beyond parliament's law-making powers.
United Nations (UN): An intergovernmental organisation aimed at promoting peace, human rights, and international cooperation. Australia is a founding Member State. The UN Charter is an international treaty binding on Australia. The UN's Universal Periodic Review process can recommend law reform to Member States.
Victorian Law Reform Commission (VLRC): Victoria's leading independent law reform organisation that reviews, researches, and makes recommendations to Victorian Parliament about possible changes to laws. Roles include inquiry (examining Attorney-General references), investigation (minor issues), monitoring law reform activity, and education.
Victorian Parliament: Consists of the King (represented by the Governor), the Legislative Council (upper house), and the Legislative Assembly (lower house).
Victorian Study Design: The VCE Legal Studies Study Design, updated periodically, which outlines the curriculum for the study.
Voting 'Along Party Lines': When members of a political party vote in the same way to pass bills, especially when their party holds a majority. This can occur even if the vote does not represent constituents' views.
World Trade Organisation (WTO): An international body comprising 164 nations that determines international trade rules. It can authorise sanctions against member countries breaching trade agreements.

Evaluate

Meaning: Make a JUDGEMENT based on criteria (e.g., Principles of Justice); weigh strengths/weaknesses; conclude [cite: 650-651, 659].

Structure (Example [cite: 659-663]):

  • Opener (Optional but recommended): State position clearly.
  • Paragraph 1 (Benefits/Strengths): Introduce point, explain, add ideas.
  • Paragraph 2 (Limitations/Weaknesses): Introduce point, explain, add ideas.
  • Conclusion: Provide overall conclusion/opinion, substantiating judgment.
Analyse

Meaning: Break down; examine relationships/impacts (how/why). Study parts in detail to determine interrelations; 'Unpack' [cite: 665-666].

Structure (Example [cite: 667-668]):

  • Identify key components/factors related to the question's focus.
  • Explain how components interrelate or connect.
  • Draw out significance, consequences, impacts (positive/negative).
Compare

Meaning: Recognise similarities AND differences between features/characteristics and their significance.

Structure (Example [cite: 681-682]):

  • Paragraph 1 (Similarities): Introduce similarity, use comparative language.
  • Paragraph 2 (Differences): Introduce difference, use contrasting language.
Distinguish

Meaning: Explain points of difference ONLY.

Structure (Example):

  • Focus sentences on points of difference. Use contrasting language (e.g., "In contrast", "Unlike").

Use analytical and signposting words to improve structure (e.g., "Therefore", "Significantly", "However", "For example") [cite: 709-711].

1. Explain the role of the Governor-General in the Commonwealth law-making process. (3 marks)

  • Grants Royal Assent to bills passed by both Houses of Parliament (final step for a bill to become law).
  • Can (theoretically/rarely) withhold Royal Assent.
  • Appoints Executive Council / Summons Parliament / Dissolves Parliament (reserve powers).

2. Distinguish between exclusive and concurrent law-making powers as outlined in the Australian Constitution, using an example for each. (4 marks)

  • Exclusive powers: Law-making powers only the Commonwealth Parliament can exercise (e.g., defence, currency, customs). States cannot legislate in these areas.
  • Concurrent powers: Law-making powers shared by both Commonwealth and state parliaments (e.g., taxation, marriage). Both can legislate in these areas.
  • Difference: Exclusive means Cth only; Concurrent means Cth and States can both make laws.
  • Difference: If inconsistency in concurrent area, s109 states Cth law prevails. No such provision needed for exclusive as only Cth legislates.
