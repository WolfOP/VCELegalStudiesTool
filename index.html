Unit 4: The People, the Law and Reform

This unit explores the institutions that make and reform laws in Australia, and the ability of individuals and groups to influence these processes[cite: 22].

Area of Study 1: The People and the Law-makers

This area of study focuses on how the Australian Constitution establishes law-making powers and acts as a check on parliament, the significance of the High Court, and the roles of parliament and courts in law-making[cite: 23, 352]. You will explore their relationship and the factors affecting their ability to make law.

This section explores the roles of the Crown and Houses of Parliament in law-making, the division of law-making powers, the significance of s109, and the impact of High Court cases.

Roles of Crown & Houses of Parliament (Vic & Cth)
  • Commonwealth Parliament: King (rep. by Governor-General), Senate (Upper House), House of Representatives (Lower House)[cite: 401, 742].
  • Victorian Parliament: King (rep. by Governor), Legislative Council (Upper House), Legislative Assembly (Lower House)[cite: 402, 906].
  • Lower Houses (House of Reps/Legislative Assembly): Initiate laws (especially money bills [cite: 73, 404]), form government, represent people, scrutinise government[cite: 404].
  • Upper Houses (Senate/Legislative Council): Act as house of review, scrutinise bills, represent states/regions, can initiate non-money bills.
  • The Crown (Governor-General/Governor): Grants Royal Assent to bills for them to become law[cite: 408, 866].
Division of Law-Making Powers
  • Exclusive Powers: Exercised only by Commonwealth Parliament (e.g., defence, currency, customs [cite: 412, 495, 773]).
  • Concurrent Powers: Shared by Commonwealth and State Parliaments (e.g., taxation, marriage, trade [cite: 413, 497, 743]).
  • Residual Powers: Powers remaining with State Parliaments, not listed in Constitution (e.g., criminal law, education, public transport [cite: 414, 499, 861]).
Significance of Section 109

If a state law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law in an area of concurrent power, the Commonwealth law prevails, and the state law is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency[cite: 55, 416, 875]. This requires a court challenge[cite: 508, 875].

High Court Cases Impacting Law-Making Powers

Cases like R v Brislan (broadened Cth power over communications) and Commonwealth v Tasmania (Tasmanian Dams Case) (Cth can legislate on matters of international treaties, impacting state residual powers) have significantly affected the division of powers.

Parliament's effectiveness in law-making is influenced by several factors[cite: 631].

  • Bicameral Structure: A 'hostile upper house' (government lacks majority) can increase scrutiny but slow law-making[cite: 59, 371, 518]. A 'rubber stamp' (government controls both houses) can mean less scrutiny but faster law-making.
  • International Pressures: Treaties and international organisations (e.g., UN) can pressure parliament to make laws complying with international standards. Failure to comply can lead to criticism[cite: 375, 453].
  • Representative Nature of Parliament (Representative Government): Parliament should reflect community views (s7 & s24 of Constitution)[cite: 61, 380, 454]. Regular elections, political parties, and independents influence this[cite: 380, 456].

The Australian Constitution acts as a check on parliamentary law-making power through several mechanisms.

  • Role of the High Court in Protecting Representative Government: The High Court interprets ss 7 and 24 of the Constitution to protect representative government[cite: 632, 592], including an implied right to freedom of political communication (ACTV case) and defining voting rights (Roach v Electoral Commissioner).
  • Separation of Powers: Divides power between legislative (parliament [cite: 64, 234, 530]), executive (government), and judicial (courts) branches to prevent abuse[cite: 63, 233, 529]. The independent judiciary can declare laws ultra vires (Australian Communist Party v Commonwealth [cite: 385, 541, 1052]). Overlap exists between legislative and executive[cite: 237, 536].
  • Express Protection of Rights: Five rights are explicitly stated (e.g., s51(xxxi) acquisition of property on just terms[cite: 67, 582], s116 freedom of religion [cite: 67, 584]). These are entrenched [cite: 238, 580] and can be enforced by the High Court[cite: 240, 581], but are limited in number and scope[cite: 240, 588].
  • Bicameral Structure: (Covered under 'Factors Affecting Parliament') The need for bills to pass both houses acts as a check[cite: 231, 550].
  • Requirement for a Double Majority in a Referendum (s128): Changing the Constitution requires approval by a national majority of voters and a majority of voters in a majority of states[cite: 244, 435, 765]. This makes constitutional change difficult[cite: 246, 436].

Courts make law through statutory interpretation and the doctrine of precedent.

Statutory Interpretation

Courts give meaning to words in legislation when applying it to a case[cite: 27, 258, 393, 892]. Reasons include clarifying ambiguity (Deing v Tarola [cite: 258, 1032]), reflecting changing word nature (A-G v Kevin and Jennifer [cite: 258, 1036]), and applying to unforeseen circumstances[cite: 258, 394, 443]. Effects include creating precedent, broadening/narrowing statutes, and prompting legislative change[cite: 260, 444, 838].

Doctrine of Precedent

Judges follow reasons for decisions (ratio decidendi) of superior courts in the same hierarchy for similar cases (stare decisis [cite: 28, 255, 890]). Features include: binding precedent (must follow [cite: 29, 736]), persuasive precedent (can guide, e.g., obiter dictum[cite: 31, 255, 831], decisions from other hierarchies [cite: 837]). Ways of developing/avoiding: reversing (same case on appeal [cite: 256, 864]), overruling (different later case by superior court [cite: 256, 832]), distinguishing (material facts different [cite: 256, 760]), disapproving (lower court disagrees but follows [cite: 256, 759]).

Several factors can influence or limit the courts' law-making role[cite: 636, 457].

  • Doctrine of Precedent: While providing consistency, it also restricts judges as they are bound by higher court decisions and must wait for a relevant case[cite: 261, 457].
  • Judicial Conservatism and Judicial Activism: Conservatism is a reluctance to make significant new law, deferring to parliament[cite: 261, 458, 810]. Activism is a willingness to develop common law considering social/political factors[cite: 261, 459, 808], which can lead to major changes but may be seen as overstepping.
  • Costs and Time: Litigation can be expensive and lengthy[cite: 33, 261, 460, 750], deterring individuals and limiting cases that come before courts[cite: 34, 461, 752].
  • Requirement for Standing (Locus Standi): Parties must have a 'special interest' and be directly affected by an issue to bring a case. This prevents frivolous cases but can also stop public interest matters from being heard[cite: 463].

Courts and Parliament have a dynamic and complementary relationship in law-making[cite: 638, 38, 446].

  • Supremacy of Parliament: Parliament is the supreme law-making body [cite: 39, 262, 447, 894] and can make or change any law within its constitutional powers, including overriding common law (except High Court constitutional interpretations [cite: 262, 450, 718]).
  • Ability of Courts to Influence Parliament: Judicial decisions, statutory interpretations, and obiter dicta [cite: 43, 262] can highlight legal issues or gaps, prompting Parliament to consider law reform[cite: 448].
  • Codification of Common Law: Parliament can enact legislation that incorporates and gives statutory authority to common law principles established by courts (e.g., Mabo case and the Native Title Act 1993 (Cth) [cite: 262, 449, 740]).
  • Abrogation of Common Law: Parliament can pass legislation that overrides or cancels common law decisions made by courts (unless the court decision is based on constitutional interpretation by the High Court [cite: 262, 450, 717]). E.g., Wrongs (Animals Straying on Highways) Act 1984 (Vic) abrogated the common law position in Trigwell's case[cite: 1048].

Abrogation of Common Law: The process of parliament overruling common law by creating a statute that is contrary to a decision of the courts[cite: 717]. Parliament can override most common law except High Court constitutional decisions[cite: 718].
Bicameral Parliament: A law-making body with two houses or chambers that must approve new bills[cite: 731]. Requirement for federal level in s1 of Constitution[cite: 369].
Binding Precedent: Legal reasoning of a higher court that must be followed by all lower courts in the same court hierarchy with similar material facts[cite: 29, 736].
Codification of Common Law: When parliament makes laws to affirm previous decisions within common law[cite: 262, 740].
Concurrent Powers: Law-making powers shared by both Commonwealth and state parliaments, listed in s51 of the Constitution (e.g., taxation, marriage)[cite: 53, 413, 743].
Exclusive Powers: Law-making powers granted only to the Commonwealth Parliament by s51 and s52 of the Constitution (e.g., currency, defence)[cite: 54, 412, 773].
Express Rights: Rights explicitly stated and entrenched in the Australian Constitution, changeable only by referendum (e.g., s116 freedom of religion).
Locus Standi (Standing): The requirement that a party must be directly affected by or have a 'special interest' in an issue to bring a case to court.
Ratio Decidendi: Latin for 'the reason for the decision'; the binding legal principle of a judgment.
Residual Powers: Law-making powers left solely with state parliaments, not listed in the Constitution (e.g., criminal law, education)[cite: 52, 414, 861].
Section 109: Section of the Australian Constitution that resolves inconsistencies between state and Commonwealth laws in concurrent areas; Commonwealth law prevails.
Separation of Powers: Constitutional principle dividing governmental powers into legislative (makes law), executive (administers law), and judicial (interprets/applies law) to prevent abuse[cite: 63, 233, 429, 881].
Statutory Interpretation: Process by which courts give meaning to words in legislation when applying it to a case[cite: 27, 258, 442, 892].
Supremacy of Parliament: Parliament is the ultimate law-making body and can make or change laws, including overriding common law (except High Court constitutional interpretations)[cite: 39, 262, 447, 894].

Understand VCAA task words and learn how to structure your exam answers effectively. Content based on guides in sources.

Explain

Meaning: Detail how/why; link cause/effect[cite: 649].

Structure (Example):

  • SIGNPOST: State main point using keywords from the question[cite: 689].
  • ELABORATE & EXAMPLE: Give specific example, use legal terms[cite: 690].
  • EXPLANATION: Detail how example supports point, consider effect/impact[cite: 691].
Discuss

Meaning: Explore multiple perspectives (pros/cons, strengths/weaknesses [cite: 650]). Present a clear, balanced argument[cite: 676].

Structure (Example [cite: 677]):

  • Paragraph 1 (Benefits/Strengths): Introduce point, explain, add ideas.
  • Paragraph 2 (Limitations/Weaknesses): Introduce point, explain, add ideas.

For 'Discuss the extent to which...': Opener stating position, supporting arguments, conflicting/limiting arguments, conclusion restating extent.

Evaluate

Meaning: Make a JUDGEMENT based on criteria (e.g., Principles of Justice); weigh strengths/weaknesses; conclude.

Structure (Example):

  • Opener (Optional but recommended): State position clearly[cite: 660].
  • Paragraph 1 (Benefits/Strengths): Introduce point, explain, add ideas[cite: 662].
  • Paragraph 2 (Limitations/Weaknesses): Introduce point, explain, add ideas[cite: 662].
  • Conclusion: Provide overall conclusion/opinion, substantiating judgment[cite: 663].
Analyse

Meaning: Break down; examine relationships/impacts (how/why [cite: 650]). Study parts in detail to determine interrelations; 'Unpack'.

Structure (Example):

  • Identify key components/factors related to the question's focus[cite: 667].
  • Explain how components interrelate or connect[cite: 667].
  • Draw out significance, consequences, impacts (positive/negative)[cite: 668].
Compare

Meaning: Recognise similarities AND differences between features/characteristics and their significance[cite: 649, 680].

Structure (Example):

  • Paragraph 1 (Similarities): Introduce similarity, use comparative language[cite: 681].
  • Paragraph 2 (Differences): Introduce difference, use contrasting language[cite: 682].
Distinguish

Meaning: Explain points of difference ONLY[cite: 649, 685].

Structure (Example [cite: 686]):

  • Focus sentences on points of difference. Use contrasting language (e.g., "In contrast", "Unlike" [cite: 686]).

Use analytical and signposting words to improve structure (e.g., "Therefore", "Significantly", "However", "For example").

1. Explain the role of the Governor-General in the Commonwealth law-making process. (3 marks) [cite: 961]

  • Grants Royal Assent to bills passed by both Houses of Parliament (final step for a bill to become law)[cite: 82, 356, 408, 866].
  • Can (theoretically/rarely) withhold Royal Assent[cite: 82, 356, 409].
  • Appoints Executive Council / Summons Parliament / Dissolves Parliament (reserve powers)[cite: 82, 202, 220, 356].

2. Distinguish between exclusive and concurrent law-making powers as outlined in the Australian Constitution, using an example for each. (4 marks) [cite: 964]

  • Exclusive powers: Law-making powers only the Commonwealth Parliament can exercise (e.g., defence, currency, customs [cite: 54, 412, 495, 773]). States cannot legislate in these areas.
  • Concurrent powers: Law-making powers shared by both Commonwealth and state parliaments (e.g., taxation, marriage [cite: 53, 413, 497, 743]). Both can legislate in these areas.
  • Difference: Exclusive means Cth only; Concurrent means Cth and States can both make laws.
  • Difference: If inconsistency in concurrent area, s109 states Cth law prevails[cite: 55, 416, 875]. No such provision needed for exclusive as only Cth legislates.
