Unit 4: The People, the Law and Reform

This unit explores the institutions that make and reform laws in Australia, and the ability of individuals and groups to influence these processes.

Area of Study 1: The People and the Law-makers

This area of study focuses on how the Australian Constitution establishes law-making powers and acts as a check on parliament, the significance of the High Court, and the roles of parliament and courts in law-making. You will explore their relationship and the factors affecting their ability to make law.

Purpose of Parliament

Parliament is the supreme law-making body within its jurisdiction. It consists of the Crown's representative and two houses.

Commonwealth Parliament

Consists of the King (represented by the Governor-General), the Senate (upper house), and the House of Representatives (lower house).

House of Representatives (Lower House)

Sometimes called the 'people's house' or the 'house of government'. It is comprised of 151 members representing electorates across Australia.

Key Roles:

  • Initiate and make laws. Most new laws are initiated here, usually by the government.
  • Determine the government. The party with the majority of members forms government.
  • Represent the people. Members are elected to represent their electorates and should reflect their views.
  • Scrutinise government administration. Includes questioning ministers.
  • Act as a house of review. Reviews bills initiated in the Senate.
  • Control government expenditure. Only the lower house can initiate money bills (taxation and appropriation).
Senate (Upper House)

Often referred to as the 'states' house' or the house of review. It has 76 elected members (12 from each state, 2 from each territory), elected for six years. It is designed for equal representation of states.

Key Roles:

  • Review laws from the House of Representatives. This is its primary role. It can amend or reject bills.
  • Allow for equal representation of the states.
  • Scrutinise bills and government administration. Often done through committees.
  • Initiate and pass bills (except money bills).
Victorian Parliament

Consists of the King (represented by the Governor), the Legislative Council (upper house), and the Legislative Assembly (lower house).

Legislative Assembly (Lower House)

Comprised of 88 members representing electoral districts. The party with the majority forms the Victorian government.

Key Roles:

  • Initiate and pass bills. Most new legislation starts here.
  • Form government.
  • Represent the people. Members represent the interests of their districts.
  • Act as a house of review. Reviews bills from the Legislative Council.
  • Control government expenditure. Initiates financial spending legislation (the Budget).
  • Scrutinise government administration.
Legislative Council (Upper House)

Primarily acts as a 'house of review'. Has 40 elected members representing eight regions.

Key Roles:

  • Act as a house of review. Reviews bills from the Legislative Assembly. It can amend or reject bills (except appropriation/money bills).
  • Scrutinise government administration.
  • Initiate and pass bills. Bills can start here, but most start in the Assembly.
The Crown (Governor-General Federally, Governor in Victoria)

The British monarch is part of the system, represented by the Governor-General and state Governors. They act on the advice of the government.

Key Roles in Law-Making:

  • Grant royal assent. This is the formal signing and approval of a bill after it has passed both houses, making it an Act of Parliament. It is required for a bill to become law.
  • Withholding royal assent. The power to refuse approval, which is rare.
  • Appointing the Executive Council. This council (leader of government, ministers) advises the Crown's representative and approves secondary legislation.

The Australian Constitution divides law-making powers between the Commonwealth and state parliaments. This division aims to distribute responsibilities and safeguard against abuse of power.

Exclusive Powers

Powers that can only be exercised by the Commonwealth Parliament. State parliaments cannot legislate in these areas. These are granted by sections 51 and 52 of the Constitution, or made exclusive by other sections or by their nature.

  • Examples: Currency, defence, customs and border protection, foreign affairs, overseas trade.
Concurrent Powers

Powers shared by both the Commonwealth and state parliaments. Both can make laws in these areas. These are listed in section 51 of the Constitution.

  • Examples: Taxation, marriage and divorce, trade, postal/telegraphic/telephonic services. Conflicts between state and Commonwealth laws in these areas are resolved by section 109.
Residual Powers

Powers that were left with the states at Federation and are not listed in the Constitution. The Commonwealth Parliament has no authority to make laws in these areas.

  • Examples: Criminal law, education, public transport, medical procedures, road laws, agriculture, police, prisons. Residual powers mean laws can differ between states.
Purpose of Section 109

Section 109 resolves conflicts between state and Commonwealth laws in areas of concurrent power.

Mechanism

If a state law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law, the Commonwealth law prevails, and the state law is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency. Only the inconsistent sections of the state law are invalid; other sections remain in force.

Significance
  • It provides a way to resolve inconsistencies between state and Commonwealth legislation.
  • It restricts the law-making powers of the states in areas where the Commonwealth has also legislated. States recognise this constraint when making laws in concurrent areas.
  • It provides a common approach for resolving conflicts.
Limitations

Section 109 does not automatically invalidate state laws; the law needs to be challenged in court for the inconsistency to be determined and the state law declared invalid. If the conflicting Commonwealth law is changed or removed, the invalid part of the state law may become valid again.

Example Case

McBain v Victoria (2000) is a significant case where the High Court used section 109 to invalidate a section of the Victorian Infertility Treatment Act 1995 that was inconsistent with the Commonwealth Sex Discrimination Act 1984, allowing single women access to IVF treatment.

The Constitution establishes structures and principles that limit Parliament's law-making power to prevent abuse.

The Bicameral Structure

The requirement for two houses (House of Representatives/Senate federally, Legislative Assembly/Legislative Council in Victoria).

  • How it Checks: The upper house acts as a house of review for bills passed by the lower house. This allows for scrutiny, debate, and potential amendments, improving the quality of laws. This check is particularly effective with a 'hostile upper house', where the government doesn't have a majority and needs crossbench support, increasing scrutiny. A deadlock between houses can also limit government legislative power.
  • Limitations: If the government holds a majority in the upper house ('rubber stamp' upper house), bills may pass with little scrutiny or debate. A hostile upper house can also cause delays and negotiations based on minority interests.
The Separation of Powers

The Constitution divides powers into legislative (parliament makes laws), executive (administers laws), and judicial (courts apply laws and resolve disputes).

  • How it Checks: Aims to prevent the abuse of power by ensuring no single body holds all three powers. The independent judiciary (High Court) can act as a check by interpreting laws and the Constitution and declaring laws made beyond Parliament's power (ultra vires) invalid. The legislature can also scrutinise the executive through processes like Question Time.
  • Limitations: There is an overlap between the legislative and executive branches in practice, as ministers are part of both Cabinet (executive) and Parliament (legislature). Judges are appointed by the executive, potentially influencing the judiciary's composition. The separation is constitutionally entrenched only at the federal level, although the principle is generally followed at the state level.
Express Protection of Rights

The Constitution explicitly protects five specific rights that are entrenched and can only be changed by a referendum (section 128).

  • How it Checks: These entrenched rights directly limit Parliament's law-making power, preventing them from making laws that infringe upon these specific rights. They are legally enforceable; individuals can challenge laws potentially breaching these rights in the High Court, which can declare them invalid.
  • Limitations: Only a limited number of rights are expressly protected (five). Some rights have limited scope and may not apply to state laws or all situations (e.g., s80 jury trial only for Commonwealth indictable offences). Changing or adding rights via referendum is very difficult and costly. Enforcing these rights through the High Court can be costly and time-consuming, requiring standing.
Role of the High Court in Protecting Representative Government

Sections 7 and 24 require Parliament to be 'directly chosen by the people', establishing representative government.

  • How it Checks: The High Court interprets sections 7 and 24 and can declare laws invalid if they infringe upon this principle. This includes recognizing implied rights necessary for representative government, such as the implied freedom of political communication.
  • Example: Roach v Electoral Commissioner (2007) is a key case where the High Court held that a complete ban on prisoners voting was unconstitutional as it infringed upon the principle of representative government under sections 7 and 24.
  • Limitations: The High Court can only act when a case is brought before it. Litigation is costly and time-consuming and requires standing. Judges are not elected, which some view as undemocratic.

The High Court interprets the Constitution and its decisions can impact the division of law-making powers between the Commonwealth and states.

R v Brislan; Ex parte Williams (1935)
  • Issue: Interpretation of 'other like services' in section 51(v) (postal, telegraphic, telephonic powers).
  • Decision: The High Court interpreted 'other like services' to include radio broadcasting.
  • Impact/Significance: This decision broadened the Commonwealth's law-making power and resulted in a shift of power to the Commonwealth in the area of communications. This interpretation has influenced the Commonwealth's assumption of power to legislate over new technologies like television and potentially the internet.
  • Limitations: The decision doesn't automatically extend to all new technologies without further cases.
Commonwealth v Tasmania (Tasmanian Dam case) (1983)
  • Issue: Interpretation of the 'external affairs' power in section 51(xxix). Could the Commonwealth use this power to legislate on a residual matter (dam construction) to implement a treaty obligation (World Heritage Convention)?.
  • Decision: The High Court held that the external affairs power allows the Commonwealth to legislate to fulfil genuine international treaty obligations, even if the subject matter is typically a state residual power. This meant the Commonwealth law prevailed over the inconsistent state law under section 109.
  • Impact/Significance: This resulted in a significant shift in the division of law-making powers from the states to the Commonwealth. The Commonwealth gained the ability to legislate on areas covered by international treaties, potentially extending to human rights.
  • Limitations: The Commonwealth cannot legislate beyond the scope of the treaty, the treaty must be genuine, and the power doesn't extinguish the state's residual power entirely.

Courts make law through the doctrine of precedent and statutory interpretation when determining cases.

Doctrine of Precedent
  • Definition: A rule where judges must follow the legal reasoning ('ratio decidendi') of decisions made by higher courts in the same court hierarchy when deciding cases with similar material facts ('stare decisis' - to stand by what has been decided).
  • Court Hierarchy: Necessary for the doctrine to operate, determining which precedents are binding.
  • Binding Precedent: The ratio decidendi of a higher court that must be followed by lower courts in the same hierarchy when material facts are similar.
  • Persuasive Precedent: Legal reasoning that is not binding but can influence a judge's decision. Can come from courts in other hierarchies, lower courts, courts of the same standing, or obiter dictum.
  • Obiter Dictum: Comments made 'by the way' in a judgment that are not part of the ratio decidendi but can be persuasive precedent.
  • Avoiding/Changing Precedent (RODD):
    • Reversing: A higher court changes the decision of a lower court in the same case on appeal. The original decision is no longer precedent.
    • Overruling: A higher court changes a precedent set by a lower court (or a court of the same standing) in a different, later case. The old precedent is no longer valid.
    • Distinguishing: A court finds the material facts of the current case are sufficiently different from a case where a binding precedent was set, allowing the court to not follow that precedent.
    • Disapproving: A court expresses disagreement with a precedent from a higher court or a court of the same standing but is still bound to follow it (obiter dictum). This doesn't change the precedent but can highlight the need for reform.
Statutory Interpretation
  • Definition: The process where courts give meaning to words or phrases in legislation when applying it to a case.
  • Reasons: Needed when words/phrases are ambiguous, undefined, or their meaning has changed over time, or to apply legislation to unforeseen circumstances not considered during drafting.
  • Effects:
    • Creates precedent: The interpretation sets a precedent for how that legislation is applied in future cases.
    • Broadens or narrows statutes: The interpretation can expand or restrict the scope and application of the legislation.
    • Prompts legislative change: Court decisions interpreting statutes can highlight issues or gaps, prompting Parliament to amend the law.

Several factors influence how effectively courts can make law:

  • The Doctrine of Precedent: Limits courts as they must wait for a relevant case to be brought before them. Judges are restricted to making comments relevant to the case (ratio decidendi). Finding relevant precedents can be timely and costly. Lower courts must follow binding precedent even if they disagree.
  • Judicial Conservatism vs Judicial Activism:
    • Judicial Conservatism: Reluctance to make new law, viewing it as Parliament's role. Tend to interpret laws narrowly. Can limit law reform.
    • Judicial Activism: Willingness to develop common law and consider social/political factors. May interpret laws more broadly. Can lead to significant legal changes (e.g., Mabo). Can be seen as undemocratic or lead to uncertainty.
  • Costs and Time in Bringing a Case to Court:
    • Costs: Legal representation, court fees, jury fees can be very high, particularly in higher courts. This can deter individuals from pursuing cases, limiting the opportunities for courts to make law. VLA support is limited.
    • Time: Cases can take months or years due to preparation, lengthy trials, and backlogs. Delays can deter litigants.
  • The Requirement for Standing: A party must be directly affected by the issue or have a 'special interest' to bring a case.
    • Effect: Ensures cases are brought by genuinely affected parties but can prevent public interest cases where no single person is uniquely impacted. Limits who can initiate action, potentially meaning important issues aren't challenged.

Courts and Parliament have a complementary relationship in law-making.

  • Supremacy of Parliament: Parliament is the supreme law-making body within its jurisdiction. It can make or change any law within its power. Parliament can abrogate (override) most common law made by courts. This ensures parliamentary laws reflect community values. Parliament cannot abrogate High Court decisions on constitutional matters.
  • Ability of Courts to Influence Parliament: Court decisions, particularly those highlighting issues with existing law or suggesting reform in obiter dictum, can influence Parliament to change the law. Cases can raise public awareness and prompt legislative action.
  • Codification of Common Law: Parliament can codify (affirm) common law principles developed by courts by passing legislation that incorporates them. This makes the law more certain and reinforces the common law principle. The Native Title Act 1993 (Cth) codified the High Court's decision in the Mabo Case (No. 2).
  • Abrogation of Common Law: Parliament can override judge-made law by passing statutes that conflict with it. This is done when Parliament disagrees with a common law decision or believes it is outdated.

Key Skill 7: High Court & Judicial Review

Understand the constitutional role of the High Court and the concept of judicial review, and how they act as checks on parliamentary power.

The Constitutional Role of the High Court of Australia (HCA)

Key roles include interpreting the Constitution, acting as the final court of appeal, and hearing certain cases directly.

  • Guardian of the Constitution (s71, s76(i)): Interprets the Constitution, ensuring Parliament acts within its powers. Decisions are final. Key in division of powers disputes (e.g., s109).
  • Final Court of Appeal (s73): Hears appeals (with special leave) from all Australian courts. Clarifies common law and ensures consistent application.
  • Original Jurisdiction (s75, s76): Hears certain cases directly (e.g., matters involving treaties, the Commonwealth as a party, or against Commonwealth officers).
The Concept of Judicial Review

Courts reviewing the lawfulness of legislative and executive actions.

  • Review of Legislation: Courts (especially HCA) determine if Acts of Parliament are constitutional. Unconstitutional laws can be declared 'ultra vires' (invalid).
  • Review of Government Decisions: Courts assess if decisions by government bodies/officials were made lawfully (e.g., within power, fair procedure).
  • Purpose: Upholds rule of law, ensures government accountability, protects rights.
Checks on Parliamentary Power

The HCA's constitutional interpretation and the process of judicial review directly limit Parliament by ensuring it doesn't legislate beyond its powers and that its laws can be struck down if unconstitutional. The independence of the judiciary is vital for this.

Explain

Clarify how or why, provide details and reasons.

Definition:

Clarify *how* or *why* something works or is significant. This often involves outlining steps, reasons, or links (e.g., explaining *one* role of the House of Representatives requires stating the role and explaining *how* it is performed).

Common Exam Pitfalls & Pro Tips

Navigate common challenges and leverage expert advice to maximize your exam performance. Click on each pitfall to reveal pro tips for overcoming it.

The Mistake: Often, students provide a description when the question asks for an evaluation, or explain when asked to analyse. This means the answer doesn't meet the specific requirements of the question.

Example of this error: For an "Evaluate the effectiveness..." question, simply listing features without making a judgment on their strengths and weaknesses.

Pro Tip(s) for Avoidance:

  • Always deconstruct the exam question first. Underline the VCAA task word(s) and any limiting words (e.g., 'one factor,' 'two means').
  • Ensure your answer structure directly addresses what the task word requires (e.g., for 'evaluate', you MUST include strengths, weaknesses, and an overall judgment).
  • Refer to the "Task Word Deep Dive" section for detailed guidance on each task word.

The Mistake: Answers are too general and lack concrete supporting evidence from relevant cases, sections of legislation, or specific real-world examples related to law reform.

Example of this error: Stating 'The High Court interprets the Constitution' without citing a relevant case like R v Brislan or the Tasmanian Dams Case when discussing division of powers.

Pro Tip(s) for Avoidance:

  • For every key legal point or argument you make, actively try to support it with a specific, relevant example.
  • When discussing the High Court's impact on the division of powers, cite cases like R v Brislan or the Tasmanian Dams Case.
  • When explaining s109, refer to a case like McBain v Victoria.
  • Create a bank of key cases (facts, decision, significance) and legislative sections as you study each topic.
  • Use the "Case Explorer" and informational sections of this hub for reference.

The Mistake: For questions worth more marks (e.g., 6, 8, or 10 marks), students may provide answers that are too brief, list points without adequate explanation, or fail to demonstrate the required level of analysis or evaluation (e.g., for 'discuss' or 'evaluate', only presenting one side of an argument or only strengths without limitations).

Example of this error: For a 10-mark 'discuss' question, providing only two brief points with minimal explanation.

Pro Tip(s) for Avoidance:

  • Use a clear structure (Introduction, Body Paragraphs with Point-Explain-Example-Link back to question, Conclusion).
  • For 'evaluate' or 'discuss the extent to which...' questions, ensure you explore multiple perspectives, weigh up strengths and weaknesses, and make a clear, substantiated judgment.
  • Each point in your body paragraphs needs to be developed with sufficient legal detail and linked back to the question's requirements.
  • Refer to the "Structured Answer Templates" (once developed) for guidance on depth and balance for different mark allocations.

The Mistake: Some questions have multiple components (e.g., 'Explain X *and* discuss its impact on Y'). Students may focus on one part and neglect the other, or not link them sufficiently.

Example of this error: If asked to 'Explain the role of the Senate and evaluate its effectiveness as a house of review,' a student might thoroughly explain the role but offer no evaluative comments on its effectiveness.

Pro Tip(s) for Avoidance:

  • Break down the question into its constituent parts during your planning time.
  • Ensure your answer explicitly addresses each identified part of the question.
  • Use signposting in your answer (e.g., 'Furthermore, regarding its impact...', 'In evaluating its effectiveness...') to show the examiner you are addressing all components.

The Pitfall: Spending too much time on low-mark questions and not enough on high-mark questions, or not finishing the exam. This can significantly impact the overall score.

Pro Tips:

  • Time per Mark: Before starting, quickly calculate how much time you can spend per mark (e.g., VCAA exams usually allow 1.5 minutes per mark, but check specific exam instructions).
  • Plan Your Attack: Quickly scan the exam. Tackle questions you're confident about first to build momentum, or start with higher-mark questions if that suits your style.
  • Stick to Allocations: Be strict with your time allocation for each question. If time is up, make a note and move on. You can come back if you have time at the end.
  • Practice Timed Exams: The best way to improve time management is to complete full practice exams under timed conditions. This helps you get a feel for pacing.

The Mistake: Students may mix up distinct but related legal terms or principles, leading to incorrect application or explanation.

Example of this error:

  • Stating that "division of powers separates the parliament, executive, and judiciary" (confusing it with separation of powers).
  • Mixing up 'concurrent powers' (shared by Cth/States) with 'residual powers' (State only).
  • Describing 'abrogation' (parliament overriding common law) when 'codification' (parliament affirming common law) is the correct term for the scenario.

Pro Tip(s) for Avoidance:

  • Create a precise glossary of key terms as you study. Pay attention to the specific definitions of terms like:
    • Separation of Powers vs. Division of Powers.
    • Exclusive vs. Concurrent vs. Residual Powers.
    • Binding Precedent vs. Persuasive Precedent.
    • Ratio Decidendi vs. Obiter Dictum.
    • Codification vs. Abrogation of common law.
  • Use concept maps or comparison tables to highlight the differences between related terms.
  • Practice applying these terms to specific scenarios to solidify your understanding. The "Interactive Glossary" and "Term Match Game" can help.

The Mistake: Students mention a relevant case name but fail to explain its specific legal reasoning, its significance to the legal principle in question (e.g., division of powers, constitutional checks), or how it applies to the question.

Example of this error:

  • Writing "The Tasmanian Dams case is important for s51(xxix)" without explaining how the High Court interpreted the external affairs power or what impact this had on the Commonwealth's ability to make laws in areas traditionally left to states.
  • Citing Roach v Electoral Commissioner but not explaining how it related to ss 7 and 24 of the Constitution or the principle of representative government.

Pro Tip(s) for Avoidance:

  • For each key High Court case, ensure you can articulate:
    1. Brief summary of the facts.
    2. The central legal issue(s) before the court.
    3. The court's decision and key aspects of its reasoning (ratio decidendi).
    4. The significance of the case, especially its impact on the interpretation of the Constitution, the division of law-making powers, or as a check on parliamentary power.
  • When using a case in an answer, explicitly link its facts, decision, or significance to the point you are making.
  • Use the "Case Explorer" and "High Court Cases & Powers" sections of this hub to deepen your understanding.

2. Distinguish between exclusive and concurrent law-making powers as outlined in the Australian Constitution, using an example for each. (4 marks)

  • Exclusive powers: Law-making powers only the Commonwealth Parliament can exercise (e.g., defence, currency, customs). States cannot legislate in these areas.
  • Concurrent powers: Law-making powers shared by both Commonwealth and state parliaments (e.g., taxation, marriage). Both can legislate in these areas.
  • Difference: Exclusive means Cth only; Concurrent means Cth and States can both make laws.
  • Difference: If inconsistency in concurrent area, s109 states Cth law prevails. No such provision needed for exclusive as only Cth legislates.
Worked Example:

1. Question Deconstruction:

  • Task Word(s): Distinguish, using an example
  • Key Content Terms: Exclusive powers, concurrent law-making powers, Australian Constitution
  • Mark Allocation: 4 marks

2. Suggested Plan/Outline:

  • Define Exclusive powers + Example.
  • Define Concurrent powers + Example.
  • Point of difference 1: Who can legislate.
  • Point of difference 2: Impact of s109 (only relevant to concurrent).

3. Full Model Answer:

Exclusive law-making powers are those that can only be exercised by the Commonwealth Parliament. An example is the power to make laws regarding currency (s51(xii) and s115 of the Constitution). Concurrent law-making powers are those shared by both the Commonwealth and state parliaments, meaning both can legislate in these areas. An example is taxation (s51(ii)).

A key difference is that only the Commonwealth Parliament can make laws in areas of exclusive power, whereas both levels of parliament can legislate in areas of concurrent power. Another difference is that if a state law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law in an area of concurrent power, section 109 of the Constitution states the Commonwealth law will prevail to the extent of the inconsistency; this is not applicable to exclusive powers as states cannot legislate in those areas at all.

  • Limitation: The process of review can be slow, lead to political deadlock, or result in compromises based on minor party demands rather than broader public interest.
  • Concluding statement: Judgement on the *extent* (e.g., a significant check, but its effectiveness is highly dependent on the political composition of the Senate).

3. Full Model Answer:

Full Model Answer (Q3):

The bicameral structure of the Commonwealth Parliament, requiring legislation to pass through both the House of Representatives and the Senate, acts as a check on parliamentary law-making to a significant extent, though its effectiveness can be influenced by the political composition of the houses.

Primarily, the Senate functions as a house of review. This means it scrutinises bills passed by the House of Representatives (where most bills originate). This review process can identify errors, ambiguities, or unintended consequences in proposed legislation, leading to amendments that improve the quality and workability of laws. For example, Senate committees often conduct inquiries into bills, allowing for expert and community input, which enhances this checking mechanism.

The effectiveness of this check is most pronounced when the government of the day does not hold a majority in the Senate (a 'hostile Senate'). In such situations, the government must negotiate with the opposition or crossbench senators to pass legislation, often leading to more thorough debate and compromise. This can prevent the government from rushing through laws without adequate consideration or pushing through measures that lack broad support.

However, the bicameral check is weakened if the government holds a majority in both houses. In this scenario, the Senate may become a 'rubber stamp', merely confirming the decisions of the lower house with limited independent scrutiny. Furthermore, while a hostile Senate can provide robust review, it can also lead to legislative deadlock or obstruction, potentially hindering the government's ability to implement its legislative agenda, even if that agenda has popular support. This can sometimes result in compromises that unduly favour minor parties holding the balance of power.

In conclusion, while the bicameral structure is a fundamental and often effective check on law-making by ensuring review and debate, its true extent of effectiveness largely depends on the political dynamics within the Senate.

3. Full Model Answer:

Full Model Answer (Q4):

One High Court case of significance involving the interpretation of sections 7 and 24 of the Australian Constitution is Roach v Electoral Commissioner (2007). These sections require that members of the Senate and House of Representatives be 'directly chosen by the people', forming the constitutional basis for representative government. In *Roach*, the High Court considered amendments to the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 which imposed a blanket ban on all sentenced prisoners from voting, regardless of sentence length.

The legal issue was whether this ban infringed the system of representative government protected by sections 7 and 24. The High Court found that the amendments were, in part, unconstitutional. While acknowledging Parliament's power to determine electoral eligibility, the Court held that this power was not unlimited and could not unduly undermine the principle of representative government. A blanket ban on all prisoners was deemed to go too far, particularly for those serving shorter sentences. The Court found that disenfranchisement must be for a 'substantial reason'.

The significance of the *Roach* case lies in its affirmation of an implied constitutional right to vote, derived from sections 7 and 24. It demonstrated the High Court's role in interpreting these foundational provisions to protect the integrity of Australia's system of representative government. Furthermore, it established that while Parliament has power to regulate voting, this power is subject to constitutional limits, and the High Court can strike down legislation that overreaches these limits. This case therefore highlighted how judicial review can act as a check on parliamentary power in the context of electoral laws.

3. Full Model Answer:

Full Model Answer (Q5):

The relationship between courts and parliament in law-making is dynamic and complementary, characterised by several distinct features. Two key features that illustrate this are the supremacy of parliament and the process of statutory interpretation by courts.

Firstly, the supremacy of parliament is a fundamental principle, establishing that parliament is the ultimate law-making authority. This means it can create or change any law within its constitutional powers and, significantly, can override (abrogate) common law made by judges. This power does not extend to High Court decisions on constitutional matters, but for other areas of common law, parliament can legislate to alter or nullify judicial precedents. For example, following the High Court's decision in State Government Insurance Commission v Trigwell (1979), which affirmed an old common law rule about straying livestock causing damage, the Victorian Parliament passed legislation to change this common law principle. This feature ensures that laws can be updated to reflect contemporary community values and that democratically elected representatives ultimately determine the state of the law, thus acting as a check on the law-making power of courts and ensuring laws are made by the elected body.

Secondly, courts play a crucial role through statutory interpretation. When applying Acts of Parliament to specific cases, courts must interpret the meaning of words and phrases within the legislation. This is vital because legislation can be ambiguous, may contain drafting errors, or may not have foreseen novel circumstances. For example, in Deing v Tarola (1993), the court had to interpret the meaning of 'regulated weapon' in a statute to determine if a studded belt fell into this category. Such interpretations by courts clarify the law, set binding precedents for how the statute is to be applied in future cases within that court's hierarchy, and can effectively broaden or narrow the application of statutes. This judicial interpretation can also highlight legislative deficiencies or unintended consequences, prompting parliament to amend the Act to better reflect its original intention or address new issues. This shows an interactive aspect of their relationship, where judicial decisions directly influence parliamentary action.

In analysing these features, it's clear that while parliament holds supreme law-making power, the courts significantly influence the application, clarification, and development of law through interpretation, creating an essential dialogue and balance between the two institutions.

Area of Study 2: The People, the Law and Reform

This area of study explores why laws may need to change, the ways individuals and groups can influence legislative change, the role of law reform bodies, and the processes and challenges of constitutional reform in Australia.

Reasons for Law Reform

Laws may need to be reformed for various reasons to ensure they remain relevant, effective, and just. Understanding these drivers is key to appreciating the dynamic nature of law.

Changes in Beliefs, Values, and Attitudes

Societal values and beliefs are not static; they evolve over time. Laws need to adapt to reflect these contemporary standards to remain relevant, enforceable, and accepted by the community. Failure to do so can lead to disrespect for the law and a perception of injustice.

Example: The legalisation of same-sex marriage in Australia in 2017, through amendments to the Marriage Act 1961 (Cth), followed years of public debate and a national postal survey indicating majority support. This reform reflected a significant shift in societal attitudes towards marriage equality and LGBTQI+ rights.

Advances in Technology

Rapid technological advancements often create new situations or behaviours that existing laws do not cover or adequately address. Law reform is needed to regulate new technologies, protect individuals from technology-related harms (e.g., cybercrime, data breaches), clarify rights and responsibilities, and facilitate innovation where appropriate.

Example: The introduction of the Online Safety Act 2021 (Cth) aimed to address issues like cyberbullying, image-based abuse, and the rapid spread of harmful online content, reflecting the need for laws to keep pace with the digital environment and its impact on individuals and the community.

Protection of the Community

A primary role of the law is to protect individuals from harm and ensure the safety and security of the community. Law reform may be necessary to respond to new threats (e.g., terrorism, pandemics), address rising crime rates in specific areas, or close loopholes in existing laws that criminals might exploit.

Example: Various state and territory governments have enacted 'one-punch' laws (e.g., NSW's Crimes Amendment (Assault and Intoxication) Act 2014) that introduce mandatory minimum sentences for fatal assaults involving alcohol or drugs, aiming to deter such violence and enhance community safety in nightlife precincts.

Greater Need for Protection of Rights

As society becomes more aware of human rights issues, or as new vulnerabilities emerge (e.g., for minority groups or those affected by new technologies), laws may need to be reformed to provide better protection for individual and group rights. This can involve creating new rights, strengthening the enforcement of existing ones, or ensuring laws comply with international human rights obligations.

Example: The ongoing discussions and recommendations for incorporating a national Human Rights Act or Charter in Australia, similar to those in Victoria (*Charter of Human Rights and Responsibilities Act 2006 (Vic)*) or the ACT, reflect a push for more explicit and legally enforceable protection of fundamental human rights at a federal level.

Improving Access to the Law

For the legal system to be fair and effective, individuals must be able to access it – to understand their rights and obligations, and to seek resolution of disputes. Law reform can aim to simplify legal processes, reduce costs, provide better information about the law (e.g., plain language initiatives), or improve the availability of legal aid and alternative dispute resolution (ADR) methods.

Example: The expansion of online portals and digital tools by courts and tribunals across Australia (e.g., the Federal Court's eLodgment system, various state-based Civil and Administrative Tribunal online services) aims to make it easier and more cost-effective for people to file documents, access information, and participate in minor dispute resolution processes without always needing expensive legal representation.

Influencing Law Reform

Various methods and entities can influence changes to the law, reflecting a democratic engagement with the legal system.

Individuals, Groups, and the Media

This section explores how different actors in society can advocate for and effect changes in the law.

Individuals

Individual citizens can be powerful catalysts for law reform. Their actions, driven by personal experience or strong conviction, can raise public awareness and place pressure on lawmakers.

  • Petitions: Collecting signatures (online or physical) to demonstrate public support for a particular change and presenting it to parliament.
  • Contacting MPs/Ministers: Writing letters, sending emails, or meeting with elected officials to voice concerns and propose reforms.
  • Protests and Demonstrations: Participating in public gatherings to draw attention to an issue and advocate for change (e.g., climate change rallies, women's marches).
  • Using Courts (Test Cases): Challenging existing laws in court, potentially leading to judicial decisions that highlight the need for legislative change or even establish new legal principles (e.g., Mabo v Queensland (No.2) (1992)).
  • Social Media Campaigns: Utilizing platforms like Twitter, Facebook, Instagram to raise awareness, share stories, organize, and mobilize support for a cause.

Example: Rosie Batty, following the tragic death of her son Luke, became a prominent advocate against family violence. Her individual campaign significantly raised national awareness and contributed to the establishment of the Royal Commission into Family Violence in Victoria and subsequent legislative and policy reforms.

Groups

Organized groups often have more resources and a louder voice than individuals, enabling them to conduct sustained campaigns for law reform.

  • Pressure Groups / Lobby Groups: These groups represent specific interests (e.g., business, unions, environmental organizations, human rights advocates).
    • Methods: Direct lobbying of politicians and public servants, preparing submissions to parliamentary committees, commissioning and publishing research, running advertising campaigns (e.g., TV, online), and making political donations (where lawful and declared).
    • Examples: The Australian Medical Association (AMA) regularly lobbies for public health reforms (e.g., tobacco control, funding for hospitals). GetUp! is an online advocacy group that campaigns on various social justice and environmental issues.
  • Community Legal Centres (CLCs) / Advocacy & Interest Groups: These non-profit organizations often work with disadvantaged or marginalized communities.
    • Methods: Providing free legal advice and representation (which helps identify systemic issues), community legal education, research into legal problems, writing submissions, strategic litigation to clarify or challenge laws, and public advocacy.
    • Examples: Environmental Justice Australia uses legal expertise to protect the environment and advocate for stronger environmental laws. The Victorian Aboriginal Legal Service (VALS) advocates for reforms to address the over-representation of Indigenous people in the criminal justice system.
The Media (Traditional and Social)

The media plays a crucial role in informing the public about legal issues and can significantly influence public opinion and the political agenda for law reform.

  • Traditional Media (Newspapers, TV, Radio): Can influence reform through investigative journalism that exposes problems, news reporting that highlights specific cases or issues, editorials that argue for change, and current affairs programs that delve into complex topics. This can hold governments accountable and create public demand for action.
  • Social Media (Twitter, Facebook, TikTok, etc.): Enables rapid dissemination of information and opinions, allows for direct communication from activists and groups, facilitates the organization of campaigns and protests (citizen journalism), and can quickly bring issues to a mass audience.

Influence: Media can be positive by raising awareness, simplifying complex issues, and giving a voice to those affected. However, it can also be negative by sensationalizing stories, creating moral panics, oversimplifying issues, exhibiting bias, or spreading misinformation, potentially leading to poorly considered law reform.

Example: ABC's "Four Corners" program has a history of investigative reports that have prompted law reform (e.g., on issues like youth detention or animal welfare). Social media campaigns such as #MeToo have globally influenced discussions and legal responses to sexual harassment and assault.

Section 128 of the Constitution outlines the strict procedure for formal constitutional change. It requires a proposed law (a Bill) to be passed by an absolute majority in both Houses of Commonwealth Parliament (or one House twice in certain circumstances).
